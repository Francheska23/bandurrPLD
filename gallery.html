STRINGS

The Bandurria is a traditional stringed instrument with deep roots in the cultural heritage of Latin America, particularly in regions such as Spain, Argentina, and Peru. Its melodious and distinctive sound comes from its rounded body and the use of metal strings, which create bright, resonant tones. The instrument is often associated with folk music and is played with a plectrum, creating an energetic rhythm that accompanies both vocal and instrumental performances.

BRIDGE

The bridge is a crucial component of the Bandurria, responsible for transmitting the vibrations from the strings to the soundboard (the body of the instrument). The bridge is positioned on the soundboard and holds the strings in place at the lower end of the instrument. The strings pass over the bridge, which is typically made of wood, and their tension is supported by the bridge as they are anchored at both ends (at the tuning pegs on the headstock and the bridge).

PEGS

The tuning pegs of the Bandurria are critical to the instrument's ability to hold and adjust string tension. Typically made from wood, plastic, or metal, these pegs are carefully designed to fit into the headstock of the Bandurria, allowing the musician to wind or unwind the strings. As the pegs are turned, they either increase or decrease the tension on each string, altering the pitch of the notes produced. The pegs are designed to be durable and provide a stable tuning that can withstand the string pressure during playing. In traditional Bandurrias, these tuning pegs are often handcrafted, reflecting the craftsmanship and heritage of the instrument.

HANDLE

The neck of the Bandurria is typically long and slender, providing the player with the reach necessary to press the strings down on the fingerboard. The neck is usually carved from wood (often hardwood like mahogany, rosewood, or maple) and is designed to be comfortable for playing, allowing the musician to access all the frets while maintaining a secure grip on the instrument.
